# Glossary of Real Estate Terms

A quick-reference guide for common and advanced terms in real estate wholesaling and creative financing.

---

### **Core Wholesaling Terms**

**After-Repair Value (ARV)**
: The estimated market value of a property *after* all necessary repairs and renovations are completed. This is the projected sale price to a retail buyer and the foundation of all deal analysis.

**Assignment Fee**
: The gross profit a wholesaler earns by assigning their contractual right to purchase a property to an end buyer. It is the difference between the wholesaler's contract price with the seller and the assignee's purchase price.

**Assignment of Contract**
: A legal instrument that transfers the rights and obligations of a purchase contract from an original buyer (the wholesaler/assignor) to a new buyer (the end investor/assignee).

**Cash Buyer**
: A real estate investor with immediate access to funds to purchase a property without traditional financing. They are the primary customers for wholesale deals due to their ability to close quickly.

**Double Close (or Simultaneous Close)**
: A closing method where two independent transactions occur back-to-back. The wholesaler first purchases the property from the seller (A-B transaction) and then immediately sells it to the end buyer (B-C transaction). This is an alternative to an assignment, often used when a contract is not assignable or to obscure the wholesale fee.

**Driving for Dollars**
: A proactive lead generation technique where investors drive through neighborhoods to identify properties showing signs of neglect or vacancy (e.g., overgrown lawns, boarded windows), which may indicate a motivated seller.

**Earnest Money Deposit (EMD)**
: A good-faith deposit made by a buyer to a seller when going under contract. It is typically held in an escrow account and applied to the purchase price at closing.

**Motivated Seller**
: A property owner with a compelling, often urgent, reason to sell their property, making them more likely to accept a below-market or creatively structured offer.

**Maximum Allowable Offer (MAO)**
: The highest price a wholesaler can offer a seller while still leaving enough profit margin for the end buyer. The standard formula is: `(ARV * 70%) - Repair Costs = MAO`.

---

### **Finance and Valuation Terms**

**Capitalization Rate (Cap Rate)**
: A rate of return used to estimate the value of an income-producing property. It is calculated as: `Net Operating Income / Current Market Value`.

**Loan-to-Value (LTV)**
: The ratio of a loan to the appraised value of an asset, expressed as a percentage. Lenders use LTV to assess risk; a higher LTV represents a higher risk.

**Net Operating Income (NOI)**
: The total income generated by a property after deducting all operating expenses (e.g., taxes, insurance, maintenance) but *before* deducting debt service (mortgage payments) or income taxes.

**Proof of Funds (POF)**
: A document, such as a bank statement or a letter from a financial institution, that demonstrates a buyer has the financial ability to complete a purchase. Wholesalers should always request a POF from their cash buyers.

---

### **Creative Financing and Advanced Terms**

**Amortization**
: The process of spreading out a loan into a series of fixed payments over time. Each payment consists of both principal and interest.

**Contingency**
: A clause in a purchase contract that must be met for the contract to become legally binding. For wholesalers, the most important contingency is the "inspection" or "due diligence" period, which allows time to find a buyer.

**Due on Sale Clause**
: A clause in a mortgage contract that requires the borrower to pay the entire remaining balance of the loan if the property is sold or transferred. This is the primary risk in a "Subject To" transaction.

**Equity**
: The difference between a property's current market value and the outstanding balance of any loans against it.

**Promissory Note**
: A written promise to pay a specified sum of money to a specified person at a specified date or on demand. This is the core document in a seller financing transaction.

**Seller Financing (or Owner Financing)**
: A transaction in which the property owner finances the purchase directly with the buyer, eliminating the need for a traditional bank loan.

**Subject To**
: A creative financing technique where a buyer takes title to a property "subject to" the existing mortgage. The buyer takes over the seller's mortgage payments, but the original loan remains in the seller's name.

---

### **Legal and Closing Terms**

**Closing**
: The final step in a real estate transaction where ownership is officially transferred from the seller to the buyer.

**Deed**
: A legal document that transfers ownership of real estate from one party to another.

**Escrow**
: A neutral third party that holds funds, documents, and other assets during a real estate transaction to ensure that all parties meet their obligations before the deal is finalized.

**Purchase and Sale Agreement**
: A legally binding contract between a buyer and a seller that outlines the detailed terms and conditions for the sale of a property.

**Title Company**
: A company that verifies the legal ownership of a property (via a title search) and issues title insurance to protect the buyer and lender from claims against the property.

### **Advanced Creative Financing Terms**

**1031 Exchange (Tax-Deferred Exchange)**
: A tax strategy that allows real estate investors to defer capital gains taxes by reinvesting proceeds from a property sale into a similar property within specified timeframes. Requires identification of replacement property within 45 days and closing within 180 days.

**Balloon Payment**
: A large lump sum payment due at the end of a loan term, typically used in seller financing arrangements. Common terms are 5-10 years with balloon payments allowing for property appreciation and refinancing.

**Blanket Mortgage**
: A single mortgage that covers multiple properties, often used in portfolio acquisitions. Requires careful allocation of debt across individual properties for subject-to transactions.

**Cash-on-Cash Return**
: A metric used to evaluate the performance of income-producing properties. Calculated as: `(Annual Cash Flow / Total Cash Invested) × 100`. Target returns typically range from 8-15% for rental properties.

**Deed in Lieu of Foreclosure**
: A legal document that transfers property ownership back to the lender without going through formal foreclosure proceedings. Used as protection in creative financing arrangements.

**Debt Service Coverage Ratio (DSCR)**
: A financial metric used by lenders to assess a property's ability to generate income sufficient to cover debt payments. Calculated as: `Net Operating Income / Annual Debt Service`. Minimum 1.25 DSCR typically required for investment property loans.

**Garn-St. Germain Act**
: Federal law (1982) that prevents lenders from enforcing due-on-sale clauses in certain situations, particularly when properties are transferred into trusts. Provides legal foundation for trust acquisition strategies.

**HUD Line 203**
: A specific line item on HUD-1 closing statements used for cash payments to sellers in subject-to transactions. Allows for proper accounting of creative financing arrangements.

**Leaseback Agreement**
: A contract where a seller becomes a tenant after closing, allowing them to remain in the property for a specified period while paying rent to the new owner. Common in situations with moving delays or family considerations.

**Performance Mortgage**
: A legal document that creates a lien against the property to ensure mortgage payments are made. Provides additional security for sellers in subject-to transactions.

**PITI (Principal, Interest, Taxes, Insurance)**
: The complete monthly housing expense including mortgage principal and interest, property taxes, and insurance premiums. Critical calculation for cash flow analysis in rental properties.

**Promissory Note**
: A written promise to pay a specified sum of money to a specified person at a specified date or on demand. This is the core document in a seller financing transaction.

**Section 8 Voucher**
: Government housing assistance program that provides rental subsidies to low-income tenants. Creates reliable rental income for investors as payments are government-guaranteed.

**Subordination Agreement**
: A legal document that changes the priority of liens against a property. Used in creative financing to establish payment priority between different financing sources.

**Trust Acquisition**
: A sophisticated property transfer method using trust structures to avoid due-on-sale clause issues while providing privacy and asset protection benefits.

**Wraparound Mortgage**
: A financing technique where a new mortgage is created that includes an existing mortgage, with the new lender making payments on the original loan while collecting payments from the borrower.

### **Real Estate Investment Metrics**

**Cap Rate (Capitalization Rate)**
: A rate of return used to estimate the value of an income-producing property. It is calculated as: `Net Operating Income / Current Market Value`.

**Cash Flow**
: The net income generated by a property after all expenses and debt service payments. Positive cash flow is essential for profitable rental property investments.

**Expense Factor**
: The percentage of rental income used for property operating expenses. Typically 20-40% for multi-family properties, including management, maintenance, vacancy, and capital expenditures.

**Gross Rent Multiplier (GRM)**
: A simple method for valuing rental properties calculated as: `Property Price / Gross Annual Rent`. Lower GRM indicates better value, typically 8-12 for most markets.

**Internal Rate of Return (IRR)**
: A financial metric that calculates the annualized return on an investment, accounting for the time value of money. Used for complex multi-year cash flow projections.

**Net Operating Income (NOI)**
: The total income generated by a property after deducting all operating expenses (e.g., taxes, insurance, maintenance) but *before* deducting debt service (mortgage payments) or income taxes.

**Return on Equity (ROE)**
: A measure of profitability calculated as: `(Net Income / Shareholder's Equity) × 100`. Used to evaluate the efficiency of real estate investments.

### **Advanced Negotiation Terms**

**Anchor Point**
: A reference price or term used to influence negotiations. In real estate, this might be the seller's asking price, recent comparable sales, or the buyer's initial offer.

**BATNA (Best Alternative to a Negotiated Agreement)**
: The most advantageous alternative course of action a party can take if negotiations fail. Understanding both parties' BATNA is crucial for successful negotiations.

**Concession Pattern**
: The strategy of making incremental concessions during negotiations to build momentum toward agreement. Effective when alternated with the other party's concessions.

**Framing Effect**
: The way information is presented influences decision-making. Real estate professionals use framing to highlight benefits and minimize perceived risks.

**Mirroring**
: A rapport-building technique where the negotiator subtly mimics the other person's body language, speech patterns, or communication style to build subconscious connection.

**Social Proof**
: The psychological phenomenon where people look to others' actions to guide their own behavior. In real estate, this includes testimonials, references, and track records.

**ZOPA (Zone of Possible Agreement)**
: The range between each party's reservation price (the least favorable point at which they would accept a deal). Successful negotiations occur within this zone.

### **Property-Specific Terms**

**Accessory Dwelling Unit (ADU)**
: A secondary housing unit on a single-family residential lot. Can significantly increase property value and rental income potential.

**Cash for Keys**
: An agreement where a property owner or tenant receives a cash payment in exchange for vacating the property by a specified date. Common in foreclosure situations.

**Days on Market (DOM)**
: The number of days a property has been listed for sale. Extended DOM often indicates pricing or condition issues and can be leveraged in negotiations.

**Highest and Best Use**
: The most profitable legal use of a property, considering zoning, market conditions, and physical characteristics. Important for value optimization.

**Off-Market Property**
: A property not publicly listed for sale but available through private networks or direct contact with owners. Often provides better acquisition opportunities.

**REO (Real Estate Owned)**
: Properties owned by banks or lenders after foreclosure. Typically sold at discount but may require extensive repairs and have title issues.

**Short Sale**
: A sale where the property is sold for less than the outstanding mortgage balance, with lender approval required. Often used when sellers are in financial distress.

**Turnkey Property**
: A fully renovated and rented property that requires no additional work from the investor. Typically includes property management and guaranteed tenants.

---

### **Legal Structure Terms**

**Deed of Trust**
: A legal document that grants a lender a security interest in a property as collateral for a loan. Used in many states as an alternative to traditional mortgages.

**Grantor**
: The person who creates a trust and transfers assets into it. In real estate trusts, this is typically the property seller establishing the trust structure.

**Land Trust**
: A legal entity that holds title to real property for the benefit of beneficiaries. Provides privacy and asset protection benefits for real estate investors.

**Living Trust**
: A trust created during a person's lifetime that becomes effective immediately. Used for estate planning and asset protection in real estate transactions.

**Quitclaim Deed**
: A legal document that transfers whatever interest the grantor has in a property without warranties. Commonly used in trust acquisitions and family transfers.

**Special Warranty Deed**
: A deed that warrants the title only against defects that arose during the grantor's ownership. Provides limited protection compared to general warranty deeds.

**Trustee**
: The person or institution responsible for managing trust assets according to the trust document terms. In real estate trusts, often a title company or attorney.

---

### **Market Analysis Terms**

**Absorption Rate**
: The rate at which available properties are sold in a specific market during a given time period. Calculated as: `(Total Sales / Total Listings) × 100`.

**Comparable Sales (Comps)**
: Recently sold properties similar to the subject property in location, size, condition, and features. Used to determine market value and ARV.

**Market Velocity**
: The speed at which properties sell in a given market. High velocity indicates strong demand, while low velocity suggests oversupply or pricing issues.

**Price per Square Foot**
: A valuation metric calculated as: `Sale Price / Property Square Footage`. Used to compare properties of different sizes within the same market.

**Supply and Demand Ratio**
: The relationship between available properties (supply) and active buyers (demand) in a market. Influences pricing and negotiation leverage.

---

### **Advanced Investment Strategies**

**BRRRR Method (Buy, Rehab, Rent, Refinance, Repeat)**
: A real estate investment strategy involving buying undervalued properties, renovating them, renting them out, refinancing to pull out equity, and repeating the process.

**House Hacking**
: A strategy where an investor lives in one unit of a multi-unit property while renting out the others to offset or eliminate housing costs.

**Opportunistic Investing**
: A high-risk, high-return strategy focusing on distressed properties, foreclosures, or market dislocations. Requires significant expertise and risk tolerance.

**Value-Add Strategy**
: An investment approach focused on properties with potential for increased value through renovations, improved management, or market repositioning.

---

### **Negotiation Psychology Terms**

**Anchoring Bias**
: The tendency to rely heavily on the first piece of information encountered when making decisions. Used strategically in real estate negotiations.

**Authority Principle**
: The psychological tendency to comply with requests from perceived authority figures. Real estate professionals leverage this through credentials and expertise.

**Commitment and Consistency**
: The principle that people strive to be consistent with their previous statements and actions. Used to build agreement incrementally in negotiations.

**Reciprocity**
: The social norm of responding to a positive action with another positive action. Real estate professionals use this by providing value before asking for concessions.

**Scarcity Principle**
: The psychological tendency to place higher value on things that are perceived as rare or limited. Used to create urgency in real estate negotiations.

**Social Validation**
: The tendency to look to others for cues on how to behave. Real estate professionals use testimonials and references to build credibility.
