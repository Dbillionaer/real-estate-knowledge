The After-Repair Value (ARV) is the most important number in your deal analysis. It's the estimated price a property will sell for on the open market *after* it has been renovated.

**The process of determining ARV is called "running comps."** This involves finding recently sold properties that are comparable to your subject property.

**What Makes a Good Comp?**
*   **Recently Sold:** Within the last 3-6 months.
*   **Proximity:** Within a 1-mile radius of your subject property (the closer, the better).
*   **Similar Size:** Within 200-300 square feet of your subject property.
*   **Similar Age:** Built within 10-15 years of your subject property.
*   **Similar Style:** e.g., Ranch vs. Colonial.
*   **Similar Features:** Number of bedrooms/bathrooms, garage, pool, etc.

**Where to Find Comps:**
*   **MLS (Multiple Listing Service):** The best source of data. You can get access through a friendly real estate agent.
*   **PropStream, DealMachine, BatchLeads:** These real estate data services have built-in comping tools.
*   **Zillow, Redfin:** Can be used for a quick-and-dirty analysis, but be aware that their data is not always 100% accurate. Look for "Recently Sold" properties.

**Process:**
1.  Find at least 3-5 comparable properties that have sold recently.
2.  Adjust for differences. If your subject property has a garage and a comp doesn't, you need to add value to the comp's sale price.
3.  Calculate the average price per square foot of your comps.
4.  Multiply that average by the square footage of your subject property to get a good estimate of the ARV.

## 2.4 Estimating Repair Costs

Accurately estimating repairs is a skill that takes time to develop. When you are new, it's always best to get a quote from a trusted contractor.

**General Rule of Thumb (for a quick estimate):**
*   **Light Rehab ($10-15/sq ft):** Mostly cosmetic. New paint, carpet, fixtures.
*   **Medium Rehab ($20-30/sq ft):** Includes light rehab plus kitchen and bath updates.
*   **Heavy Rehab ($40+/sq ft):** Major systems need replacement (roof, HVAC, plumbing, electrical) and/or the layout is being changed.

**Big Ticket Items (The "Big 5"):**
These are the most expensive items to replace. Always check their condition.
1.  **Roof:** Look for curling or missing shingles.
2.  **HVAC (Heating, Ventilation, and Air Conditioning):** Check the age of the unit.
3.  **Plumbing:** Look for leaks or signs of water damage.
4.  **Electrical:** Look for an outdated panel or exposed wiring.
5.  **Foundation:** Look for major cracks in the walls or foundation.

## 2.5 Calculating the Maximum Allowable Offer (MAO)

The MAO is the highest price you can offer the seller that still leaves enough profit in the deal for your cash buyer (and you!).

**The Standard MAO Formula:**

`(ARV * Investor's Percentage) - Repair Costs - Your Desired Assignment Fee = MAO`

*   **Investor's Percentage:** Most cash buyers (fix-and-flippers) will not pay more than 70-75% of the ARV for a property, as they need to account for their own holding costs, closing costs, and profit margin. Always be conservative and use 70% in your initial calculation.
*   **Your Desired Assignment Fee:** This can range from $5,000 to $20,000 or more, depending on the deal.

**Example:**
*   ARV: $200,000
*   Repair Costs: $30,000
*   Your Desired Fee: $10,000

`($200,000 * 0.70) - $30,000 - $10,000 = $100,000`

In this scenario, your Maximum Allowable Offer to the seller is **$100,000**.
