## Overview
This comprehensive playbook covers all aspects of subject-to deal acquisition, from initial outreach to closing. Based on proven strategies from recorded calls and email templates.

## 📞 **SUBJECT-TO PHONE SCRIPTS**

### **Foundation Subject-To Pitch**
**Recording Reference**: `Subject To Pitch 1-3`

#### **Market Context & Seller Psychology**
Subject-to deals work best with sellers facing **financial distress** but who still have **equity and pride** in their property. These sellers are typically 60-120 days behind on payments, facing foreclosure, but emotionally attached to their home. The psychology centers on **credit preservation** and **dignity maintenance** - they want to avoid the shame of foreclosure while protecting their financial future.

**Optimal Property Characteristics:**
- 2-6 months behind on payments
- $20K+ equity remaining
- Good payment history before recent troubles
- Owner-occupied (emotional attachment)
- Loan balance under 80% of current value

**Seller Motivation Indicators:**
- Recent job loss or income reduction
- Medical bills or family emergency
- Divorce or family crisis
- Business failure
- Adjustable rate mortgage payment increases

#### **The Setup with Psychological Framework**
```
"Hi [Name], my name is [Your Name] and I'm calling about your property at [Address].
I have a unique solution that might help your situation. Instead of you going through
foreclosure or damaging your credit, I can take over your mortgage payments and get
your name off the liability. Let me explain how this works..."
```

**Why This Works:** "Unique solution" creates curiosity and hope. "Instead of foreclosure" immediately addresses their biggest fear. "Get your name off the liability" offers the relief they desperately want.

**Tone:** Confident but compassionate, speaking slightly slower than normal to ensure comprehension during their stressful state.

#### **The Explanation Framework with Timing**
1. **Problem Identification** (2 minutes): Current payment struggles
   - "Help me understand your situation..."
   - Listen for emotional triggers and financial details
   - Identify timeline pressure and consequences they're facing

2. **Solution Introduction** (3 minutes): Payment takeover concept
   - Use simple analogies: "Like assuming a car loan, but for houses"
   - Address legal concerns upfront: "Everything is done through attorneys"
   - Emphasize win-win nature: "This helps both of us"

3. **Benefit Emphasis** (2 minutes): Credit protection, stress relief
   - Quantify credit impact: "Foreclosure drops credit scores 200+ points"
   - Paint picture of relief: "Imagine sleeping without payment worries"
   - Timeline benefits: "Stop foreclosure process immediately"

4. **Process Overview** (3 minutes): Deed transfer, payment assumption
   - Step-by-step walkthrough
   - Role of attorneys and title companies
   - Timeline from agreement to closing

5. **Next Steps** (2 minutes): Property evaluation, paperwork
   - "What questions do you have so far?"
   - Schedule property visit
   - Explain due diligence process

#### **Key Benefits with Psychological Triggers**
- **Credit Protection**: "Saves your credit score and your financial future"
  - *Psychology*: Addresses long-term consequences they're terrified of
  - *When to emphasize*: When they mention credit concerns or future loan needs

- **Stress Relief**: "No more sleepless nights worrying about payments"
  - *Psychology*: Addresses immediate emotional pain they're experiencing
  - *When to emphasize*: When they sound exhausted or overwhelmed

- **Quick Solution**: "Can start payments immediately and stop foreclosure"
  - *Psychology*: Provides immediate hope and action plan
  - *When to emphasize*: When foreclosure timeline is pressing

- **Win-Win**: "You get relief, I get property, bank gets payments"
  - *Psychology*: Removes guilt about "taking advantage" of their situation
  - *When to emphasize*: When they seem hesitant about fairness

#### **Advanced Objection Handling for Subject-To**

**1. "This sounds too good to be true"**
- **Response**: "I understand that skepticism - when you're in a tough spot, everything can feel like a scam. Here's the reality: I make money by taking over properties with good bones and equity. You benefit by avoiding foreclosure. The bank benefits by getting payments. It's not too good to be true, it's just a different way of doing business. Let me show you exactly how it works and connect you with other sellers I've helped."
- **Psychology**: Validates skepticism while explaining business logic
- **Proof**: Provide references and detailed explanation of your business model

**2. "What if you stop making payments?"**
- **Response**: "That's a smart question and shows you're thinking this through. Here's my guarantee: I'll provide you with a performance mortgage that legally requires me to make payments. If I ever miss a payment, you get the house back immediately. Plus, I'll show you my track record - I've never missed a payment because my business depends on my reputation."
- **Legal protection**: Explain performance mortgages and legal safeguards
- **Track record**: Provide payment history and references

**3. "My attorney says this is risky"**
- **Response**: "Your attorney is looking out for you, which is exactly what they should do. Many attorneys aren't familiar with subject-to transactions because they're not common. I work with real estate attorneys who specialize in these deals. Would it help if my attorney spoke with yours to explain the legal protections we put in place?"
- **Respect**: Never criticize their attorney
- **Education**: Offer attorney-to-attorney consultation

**4. "I don't understand how you make money"**
- **Response**: "Great question - transparency is important. I make money in three ways: First, I rent the property for more than the mortgage payment. Second, I improve the property over time, building equity. Third, I eventually refinance or sell the property. I'm essentially buying your equity over time while solving your immediate problem."
- **Transparency**: Explain your business model clearly
- **Timeline**: Show how you profit long-term, not immediately

**5. "What about the due-on-sale clause?"**
- **Response**: "You're well-informed - that's a real consideration. Here's what I've learned in [X] years of doing this: Banks rarely enforce the due-on-sale clause when payments are being made on time. Their goal is to get paid, not to foreclose. That said, there is some risk, which is why I price my offers to account for that possibility."
- **Honesty**: Acknowledge the risk exists
- **Experience**: Share your track record with due-on-sale situations
- **Risk mitigation**: Explain how you handle this possibility

**6. "I need to talk to my spouse first"**
- **Response**: "Absolutely - this affects both of you and you should make this decision together. What concerns do you think your spouse will have? I can provide information that helps you explain this to them. Would it be helpful if I spoke with both of you together to answer any questions?"
- **Psychology**: Respects relationship dynamics while moving process forward
- **Strategy**: Offer three-way conversation to address spouse concerns directly

**7. "How do I know you won't trash the property?"**
- **Response**: "That's a legitimate concern - this is your home and it matters to you. I can provide references from neighbors of properties I manage, before and after photos of improvements I've made, and a written agreement about property maintenance standards. I have a reputation to protect in this community."
- **Emotional connection**: Acknowledge their attachment to the property
- **Proof**: Provide visual evidence of your property care standards

**8. "What happens if I want to buy it back someday?"**
- **Response**: "I appreciate that emotional connection to your home. While I can't guarantee a buyback option, I can include a right of first refusal in our agreement. If I ever decide to sell, you'll get the first opportunity to purchase it back at fair market value. That way, there's always a possibility of it returning to your family."
- **Psychology**: Addresses emotional attachment and hope for future recovery
- **Legal option**: Explain right of first refusal and how it works

#### **Transition Techniques for Subject-To**

**From Skepticism to Trust:**
"I can hear the doubt in your voice, and that's actually smart - you should be cautious. Let me share something that might help..." (Share specific success story with similar situation)

**From Fear to Hope:**
"I know this feels scary - you're already dealing with so much stress. What if I told you that three months from now, you could be sleeping peacefully knowing your credit is protected and this burden is off your shoulders?"

**From Confusion to Clarity:**
"I can tell this is a lot to process. Let me break it down into simple steps..." (Use visual aids or simple analogies)

#### **Optimal Call Flow for Subject-To (15-20 minutes)**
- **Rapport Building**: 2-3 minutes (find common ground, show empathy)
- **Problem Discovery**: 4-5 minutes (understand their situation deeply)
- **Solution Presentation**: 5-6 minutes (explain subject-to process)
- **Objection Handling**: 3-5 minutes (address concerns thoroughly)
- **Close & Next Steps**: 2-3 minutes (schedule property visit, next call)

**Critical Pacing Elements:**
- Speak 15% slower than normal (they're processing complex information)
- Pause 5-7 seconds after explaining key concepts
- Use their name frequently to maintain connection
- Match their emotional state initially, then gradually lift their energy

## 📧 **SUBJECT-TO EMAIL TEMPLATES**

### **Basic Subject-To Email Template**
```
Good Afternoon [Realtor Name],

Thank you for taking time out of your day to speak with me today about your listing on [Address]. Based on public record, if an offer came in at full ask, your client would walk away with [Net amount]. That's with no concessions and a full price offer.

As an alternative, I'd like to offer your client [Premium amount] + title fees for us to take over their position in their [Mortgage amount] mortgage. Your commission comes out of the [Premium amount] but all other closing costs are paid for by us on top of the [Premium amount].

If payments are missed for a period of 2 months the property is to be deeded back to your client without them having to foreclose. We'll accomplish this with a deed of trust that we attach to our purchase and sale agreement.

Regards,
[Your Name]
```

### **Trust Acquisition Email Template**
```
Good Evening [Realtor Name],

Thank you for taking my call earlier today on [Address]. I was initially going to make a cash offer on this one but while we were on the phone we discovered that your client wasn't making very much from the sale.

I have another closing happening tomorrow. In this deal, we accomplish the same outcome as we would on a subject to deal but without the risks to your client. Essentially, the seller of [Example Address] owes near what they're asking for the property.

So we opened a trust with the seller and purchased the controlling interest of the trust from them. The due on sale clause cannot be called on properties within a trust according to the Garn St. Germain Act of 1981.

My offer would actually come in significantly over list. I'd offer [Cash amount] cash right now to take over their position in the loan, and another [Balloon amount] on year [X] as a balloon payment.

- Buy price: [Total amount]
- Down Payment: [Cash amount]
- All closing costs covered by us
- Balloon Term: [X] years
- Balloon Payment: [Balloon amount] on year [X]

If we miss payments, the property is deeded back to your client. Your commission comes out of the [Cash amount] cash at closing.

Regards,
[Your Name]
```

### **Multi-Family Subject-To Template**
```
Good Evening [Realtor Name],

Thank you for taking time out of your day to speak with me about your multi unit in [City]. I've included my business partner, [Partner Name] in this email. He owns property in [City] on seller financing agreements like the one we're discussing.

[Partner Name] and I view deals like this as purchasing cashflow. Our down payment is proportionate to how much we receive on a monthly basis.

If you receive around [Monthly income], my expenses look like this: [Income] x .20 for vacancy, repairs, and management = [Net after expenses]. I also have taxes and insurance of [Amount]/month + another [Mortgage amount] for the mortgage payment. That leaves us with [Net cashflow]/month in NET cashflow.

I have to ask myself how much I'm willing to put down for this amount of cashflow. Unfortunately, I haven't accounted for a monthly payment to you. If I kept all [Net cashflow] of cashflow I could put down [Down payment amount] as initially offered.

We discussed making payments of [Payment amount]/month but the property makes [Income] based on the numbers you gave me. It would be a challenge to put [Down payment] down and pay you [Payment amount] month for a return of just [Return amount]/month.

Please tell me if you see error in how I've come up with the net income for this property.

- [Purchase price] Buy Price
- [Down payment] Down Payment
- Mortgage of [Mortgage amount] taken over in full + taxes and insurance
- Out of [Income]/month in income, I'll pay [Payment amount] (the majority) to your client
- These payments will be interest only at [Rate]%
- We'll sell on or before the date of the [X] year balloon paying out all proceeds to your client

Let me know what your client's thoughts are.

Regards,
[Your Name]
```

## 🎭 **SUBJECT-TO ROLE-PLAY SCENARIOS**

### **Scenario C: The Distressed Landlord (Subject-To)**
**Background**: Mike, 52, owns three rental properties. Lost his job six months ago, behind on payments on all properties. Facing foreclosure in 60 days. Proud, doesn't want to admit failure.

**Property Situation**:
- 3BR/2BA rental property
- Good condition, currently vacant
- $1,200/month rental potential
- $980/month mortgage payment
- $15K behind on payments
- Property value: $165K, loan balance: $125K

**Expected Responses**:
- "I just need to find a tenant and I'll be fine"
- "This is temporary - my situation will improve"
- "I don't want to lose my investment properties"
- "How do I know you won't just flip it for profit?"

**Training Focus**:
- Acknowledge his business acumen and experience
- Focus on credit protection and future opportunities
- Show how this preserves his ability to invest again
- Emphasize partnership rather than bailout

**Success Metrics**:
- Conversion rate: 45% (pride barrier)
- Timeline: 2-4 weeks (urgency vs. pride)
- Key: Respect his expertise while showing solution

### **Scenario E: The Desperate Homeowner (Subject-To)**
**Background**: Lisa, 34, single mother, lost job due to company downsizing. Three months behind on mortgage, foreclosure notice received. Emotional, scared, but trying to keep it together for her kids.

**Property Situation**:
- 3BR/2BA starter home
- $145K value, $128K loan balance
- $1,050/month payment
- $3,150 behind on payments
- Foreclosure sale in 45 days

**Expected Responses**:
- "I can't lose my children's home"
- "I just need more time to find a job"
- "This sounds too good to be true"
- "What if my kids' friends find out?"

**Training Focus**:
- Lead with compassion and understanding
- Focus on protecting her children's stability
- Emphasize dignity and fresh start opportunity
- Provide immediate hope and action plan

**Success Metrics**:
- Conversion rate: 85% (high desperation)
- Timeline: 1-2 weeks (foreclosure pressure)
- Key: Emotional support and immediate relief

## 🌍 **MARKET ADAPTATIONS FOR SUBJECT-TO**

### **High-Foreclosure Markets (Post-Economic Stress)**
- Emphasize speed and immediate relief
- Provide more foreclosure timeline education
- Share more success stories from similar situations
- Focus on credit protection benefits

### **Stable Markets (Individual Financial Crisis)**
- Focus on dignity and privacy preservation
- Emphasize temporary nature of their situation
- Highlight future financial recovery possibilities
- Provide more detailed financial analysis

### **Luxury Markets (High-Value Properties)**
- Use more sophisticated language and concepts
- Provide detailed legal structure explanations
- Emphasize asset protection strategies
- Involve attorneys earlier in the process
