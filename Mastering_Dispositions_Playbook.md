# Module 7: Mastering Dispositions Playbook

## 7.1 From Reactive to Proactive: The Dispositions Mindset

For most new wholesalers, "dispositions" is a reactive process: you get a deal under contract, and then you frantically blast it out to every buyer you know, hoping someone bites.

An elite dispositions process is **proactive**. It's about building such strong relationships with a core group of buyers that you can sell your deals with a single phone call *before* they ever go out to your general list. It's about knowing exactly what your buyers want and delivering it to them consistently.

This playbook will teach you how to move from a reactive to a proactive dispositions model, maximizing your profit and minimizing your risk.

## 7.2 Buyer Archetypes: Understanding Who You're Selling To

Not all cash buyers are the same. To market your deals effectively, you need to understand the different "archetypes" of buyers and what motivates them.

### The "Fix-and-Flip" Buyer

This is the most common type of buyer. They are looking for properties they can renovate and sell for a profit on the retail market.

*   **What they care about:** The After-Repair Value (ARV) and the cost of repairs. They live and die by the 70% rule (or a similar formula).
*   **How to market to them:** Your marketing should be all about the numbers. Provide a clear, conservative ARV, a detailed repair estimate, and photos of the property.
*   **What they need from you:** Accurate numbers and quick, easy access to the property for their own inspection.

### The "Buy-and-Hold" Landlord

This buyer is looking for properties to add to their rental portfolio. They are focused on long-term cash flow.

*   **What they care about:** The potential rental income, the condition of major systems (roof, HVAC, plumbing), and the quality of the neighborhood.
*   **How to market to them:** In addition to the ARV and repairs, provide rental comps (what similar properties are renting for). Highlight features that make it a good rental, like a durable roof or a low-maintenance yard.
*   **What they need from you:** Information about the neighborhood, school districts, and potential rental income.

### The "Wholetailer" Buyer

This buyer is another wholesaler, but they have a strong retail or construction arm. They are looking for deals that are "too good" for a standard flip but need some work before being listed on the MLS. They might do a light cosmetic rehab and then list the property.

*   **What they care about:** A deep discount. They need enough room in the deal to do a light rehab, pay realtor commissions, and still make a profit.
*   **How to market to them:** Focus on the huge equity spread. "ARV is $300k, needs $20k in paint and carpet, and you can get it for $220k."
*   **What they need from you:** A great price and a quick, easy transaction.

By segmenting your buyers list into these archetypes, you can send targeted marketing messages that speak directly to their needs, dramatically increasing your response rate and your ability to sell deals quickly.

## 7.3 The Art of the Assignment Fee: Pricing Your Deals

How much should you charge for an assignment fee? It's a question that plagues many new wholesalers. The answer is: it depends. Your fee is not a fixed number; it's a dynamic price based on the quality of the deal and the buyer you're selling to.

**The "Greedy Wholesaler" vs. The "Smart Wholesaler"**

*   **The Greedy Wholesaler:** Tries to squeeze every last dollar out of every deal. They might make a huge fee on one deal, but they burn bridges with buyers who feel like they overpaid.
*   **The Smart Wholesaler:** Knows that a quick nickel is better than a slow dime. They price their deals fairly, leaving plenty of profit for the end buyer. They build a reputation for bringing good deals, and buyers line up to work with them.

**Pricing Strategies:**

*   **The "Slam Dunk" Deal (Deep Discount):** If you have a property under contract for a truly incredible price (e.g., 50% of ARV), you can charge a larger assignment fee ($20,000, $30,000, or even more).
*   **The "Good" Deal (Standard Discount):** For most deals, a standard assignment fee of $5,000 to $15,000 is appropriate. This leaves enough profit in the deal to make it attractive to a wide range of buyers.
*   **The "Skinny" Deal (Low Equity):** Sometimes you'll get a deal under contract that doesn't have a huge equity spread. In these cases, it's better to make a small fee ($2,500 - $5,000) and sell the deal quickly than to hold out for a bigger payday and risk losing the deal altogether.

**Handling Objections to Your Fee:**

Your buyer will see your assignment fee on the closing statement. Be prepared to justify it.

*   **Buyer:** "You're making $15,000 on this? That's too much."
*   **You:** "I understand it might seem like a lot, but remember, I spent thousands of dollars on marketing and countless hours on the phone to find this deal. You are getting an off-market property with a significant discount, and my fee is the cost of that acquisition. The numbers work for you, right?"

Focus the conversation on the value you are providing and the quality of the deal, not on your fee.

## 7.4 Joint Venturing (JV) Done Right: Expanding Your Reach

What happens when you have a deal under contract, but you can't find a buyer on your list? This is where Joint Venturing (JVing) comes in. A JV is simply a partnership with another wholesaler to sell a deal.

**The Two Roles in a JV:**

1.  **The "Boots on the Ground":** The wholesaler who has the property under contract.
2.  **The "Money Guy" (or "Buyer Bringer"):** The wholesaler who has the cash buyer.

**How it Works:**

1.  You have a deal you can't sell.
2.  You reach out to another wholesaler with a large buyers list.
3.  You sign a **JV Agreement** that outlines how you will split the assignment fee (typically 50/50).
4.  The other wholesaler markets the deal to their list and finds a buyer.
5.  At closing, the title company splits the assignment fee between the two of you according to the JV Agreement.

**Keys to a Successful JV:**

*   **Work with people you trust.** Your reputation is on the line.
*   **Get it in writing.** Always use a JV Agreement to avoid any misunderstandings.
*   **Be transparent.** Both partners should be clear about their roles and responsibilities.

JVing is a powerful tool. It allows you to tap into other wholesalers' buyers lists, sell more deals, and build relationships in the investor community. By mastering these disposition strategies, you will build a reputation as a reliable source of profitable deals, ensuring your long-term success in the wholesaling business.

## 7.5 Buyer Qualification: Vetting Before You Send Deal Details

Never send full deal details (address, price, photos) to an unqualified buyer. Unqualified buyers waste your time and can tie up your deal.

### Buyer Qualification Questions

Ask these before sending deal details:

1. **"What type of properties are you buying right now?"** — Confirms they're active
2. **"What's your typical price range?"** — Confirms the deal fits their criteria
3. **"Are you buying cash or using financing?"** — Cash buyers close faster and more reliably
4. **"How quickly can you close?"** — You need to know if they can meet your contract deadline
5. **"Can you send me proof of funds?"** — Non-negotiable for serious buyers

### Proof of Funds (POF) Requirements

| Buyer Type | Acceptable POF |
|-----------|---------------|
| Cash buyer | Bank statement showing liquid funds ≥ purchase price |
| Hard money buyer | Pre-approval letter from hard money lender |
| Private money buyer | Letter from private lender confirming available funds |

**POF Red Flags:**
- Refuses to provide POF ("I'll show you at closing")
- POF is from a business account with no clear connection to the buyer
- POF shows funds that are clearly insufficient for the deal
- POF is dated more than 30 days ago

### Buyer Tier System

Maintain a tiered buyers list:

| Tier | Criteria | Priority |
|------|----------|----------|
| **VIP** | Closed 2+ deals with you, POF verified, responds within 2 hours | First call, 24-hour exclusive window |
| **Active** | Closed 1 deal or bought from another wholesaler recently, POF verified | Second wave, 48-hour window |
| **Prospect** | New buyer, POF not yet verified | General blast after VIP/Active |
| **Inactive** | Haven't bought in 6+ months | Last resort or remove from list |

- **"Price drops $5K every 48 hours until sold"** — Escalating urgency (use carefully)
- **"VIP buyers get 24-hour exclusive access"** — Rewards your best buyers

## 7.7 When a Buyer Backs Out After Signing

If a buyer signs the Assignment Agreement and then backs out:

1. **Check the Assignment Agreement** — Is their EMD forfeitable?
2. **Determine the reason** — Inspection issue? Financing fell through? Cold feet?
3. **Negotiate** — Can you reduce the price to keep the deal alive?
4. **Activate backup buyer** — Always have a backup buyer lined up before closing
5. **Re-blast the deal** — You still have the property under contract; find a new buyer
6. **Document everything** — If you need to pursue the forfeited EMD, you'll need a paper trail

**Prevention:** Always collect a non-refundable assignment fee deposit ($1,000–$2,500) when the Assignment Agreement is signed. This filters out non-serious buyers.
